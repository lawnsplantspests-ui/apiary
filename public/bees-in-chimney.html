What you'll notice

How chimney colonies announce themselves

Usually one of three ways: a halo of bee traffic around the chimney top on warm days; the occasional confused bee appearing in the fireplace or on nearby windowsills (they wander down the flue and follow light into the room); or — in summer — a faint honey smell near the fireplace. If bees showed up in the firebox itself, the colony is in the flue above you.

The comb usually hangs in the upper portion of the flue or under the crown, anchored to the tile or masonry. Colonies that have been in a chimney for a year or more can carry astonishing amounts of comb — chimneys are the "hollow trees" of the neighborhood, and bees treat them accordingly.

Please don't

Three things not to do

  • Don't light a fire. It's the folk remedy everyone reaches for, and it's the worst one. Smoke doesn't evict an established colony — but heat melts the comb, sending wax and honey down your flue (a genuine chimney-fire fuel), and panicked bees pour down into the house instead of up and out.
  • Don't cap or screen the flue while they're in there. A trapped colony doesn't die quietly — it follows air and light, and the exit it finds is your fireplace damper. Capping is the last step, after removal, and then it's the single best thing you'll ever do for that chimney.
  • Don't dump pesticide down the flue. Beyond killing a relocatable colony: dead bees and abandoned honey above your damper rot, drip, reek, and pull in ants, mice, and every robber bee in the neighborhood. And your fireplace now has pesticide in it.
How we do it

How chimney bee removal works

  1. Assess from the roof. We confirm honey bees, find where the comb sits in the flue, and figure out the access — from the top, through the firebox, or occasionally both.
  2. Protect the house. The damper gets sealed off and the firebox masked, so the work stays in the chimney and out of your living room.
  3. Remove bees and comb. Bees are vacuumed alive with the queen caged; comb comes out section by section. Brood comb travels with the colony to our apiary; honey comes out so nothing is left to melt or rot above your damper.
  4. Clean the flue area we worked and check for residue. (A full chimney sweep afterward is smart anyway — we'll tell you honestly whether the job left anything worth sweeping.)
  5. Cap the chimney. A proper cap ends the chimney's career as bee real estate — and keeps out the squirrels, birds, and raccoons too. If wildlife is already part of your chimney's story, our sister company Lawns Plants & Pests handles that side.
Questions

Chimney bee FAQ

Bees keep showing up in the fireplace — is that the colony coming in?

No — those are stragglers wandering down the flue and following light into the room. It's a nuisance, not an invasion, but it's also your early warning: the colony above is active. Keep the damper closed and give us a call.

Can you just smoke them out?

No — that's not what smoke does. Beekeeper's smoke calms bees; it doesn't evict them. An established colony will not abandon brood and honey because of smoke, no matter how much of it comes up the flue. Removal means removing.

How long has the colony been up there?

We can usually tell at the assessment: a swarm that arrived weeks ago has a few palm-sized combs; a year-old colony has comb by the foot and honey by the bucket. It changes the scope, which is why estimates happen on site and are free.

Do I need a mason afterward?

Rarely. Most chimney removals work within the existing flue and finish with a standard cap. If we find crown or masonry damage (usually pre-existing — it's often how the bees got comfortable in the first place), we'll show you photos and you can bring your mason in informed.
