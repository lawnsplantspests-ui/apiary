What a swarm looks like

A swarm is a temporary cluster of honey bees with their queen, hanging from something outside — a branch, a fence post, a porch column, a mailbox. The cluster looks like a hanging fist-to-football of bees, calm, mostly motionless. There's no comb, no honey, no babies inside the cluster — just bees, food in their stomachs, and a queen.

Swarms are temporary. The bees are looking for a new home. They'll stay clustered for a few hours to a couple of days while scout bees explore the neighborhood, then leave together.

What an established colony looks like

An established colony lives inside something — a wall void, a soffit, an old hollow tree, a chimney chase, an attic, a shed wall. You'll see bees flying steadily in and out of a single hole or seam, the same hole every day, for weeks or months. There's no visible cluster. The bees are inside, and inside they've built comb full of honey and brood.

Established colonies don't move on. They expand. A colony that's been in a wall for a year can have 60+ pounds of honey and tens of thousands of bees living between your siding and your drywall.

Not all flying-yellow-and-black is honey bees

Yellow jackets and bald-faced hornets are the biggest source of mistaken-identity calls during stinging-insect season. The fastest way to tell:

  • Honey bees are golden-brown and fuzzy. Body is rounded. They die after they sting.
  • Yellow jackets are bright yellow and black, shiny (not fuzzy), with a narrow waist. They can sting repeatedly. Often nesting in the ground or in walls.
  • Bald-faced hornets are black and white. Big — up to 3/4 inch. Nests look like grey paper footballs hanging from trees or eaves.

Why this matters

The biggest mistake homeowners make is treating an established wall colony like a swarm — or treating a swarm like a pest problem. Spraying a swarm is unnecessary and wasteful (they were going to leave anyway). Trying to "wait out" an established wall colony lets the honey stores grow until removal becomes a much bigger job.
