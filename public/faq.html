Removal & swarms

Honey bee removal & swarms

Will you remove honey bees from my house for free?

Free for accessible swarms (clusters of bees hanging in the open). Established colonies inside walls, soffits, or chimneys are quoted on-site after we see the situation. See our Bee Removal page for full details.

How fast can you respond to a swarm?

Same-day during peak swarm season (April through early July) whenever possible. Don't wait — swarms move on within 24–72 hours.

Do you kill any honey bees?

No. Live removal and relocation only. If a colony can't safely be relocated, we tell you up front and discuss options.

What about bumble bees?

We relocate bumble bee colonies to our apiary rather than killing them. Most pest control companies kill them. Most beekeepers won't touch them. See Bee Removal for the bumble bee relocation section.

What if I don't have honey bees — what if it's yellow jackets or hornets?

Send a photo to 717-379-3248 — we identify for free. If it's a stinging insect that isn't honey bees, our sister company Lawns Plants & Pests LLC handles those professionally.

Why is my honey crystallized?

Crystallization is a sign of real, raw honey. To re-liquify, place the jar in warm (not hot) water for 15–30 minutes. Don't microwave. See our article on why raw honey crystallizes.

Do you ship honey?

No, pickup only at our honey stand. We're a local apiary, and we'd rather have you taste it in person.

Is your honey organic?

The honest answer: nobody producing honey in the eastern U.S. can legitimately claim "organic" the way the term is used for produce. Honey bees forage up to 3 miles from the hive — we can't control where 50,000 bees decide to fly. Be skeptical of anyone in PA selling "organic honey."

For beekeepers

Nucs & queens

When can I buy nucs?

Spring — typically late April through June, weather permitting. Reservations open in fall with a 50% deposit. See Nucs & Queens.

What kind of bees are they?

Mixed Northern survivor stock — Carniolan, Italian, Russian, and local genetics selected for PA conditions. Not pure-breed; they're working bees adapted to Pennsylvania.

Do you ship nucs or queens?

No, pickup only at our apiary in Harrisburg, PA.

What's the difference between a package and a nuc?

A package is loose bees with a caged queen — no comb, no brood, no food. A nuc is a small, established working colony with all of that. Nucs install faster, build up faster, and have a much higher success rate.
