What you're looking at

Why they're hanging there

When a strong colony outgrows its home in spring, it splits: the old queen leaves with roughly half the bees to found a new colony. That traveling half is the swarm. They cluster somewhere convenient — a branch, a shrub, a swing set — while scout bees fly out searching for a permanent cavity. The cluster is a waiting room, not a nest.

And here's the thing about swarming bees: they have nothing to defend. No comb, no brood, no honey stores — and they gorged on honey before leaving home, which makes them about as docile as honey bees ever get. Swarms look terrifying and sting almost nobody. We handle many of them without so much as a veil. (Still — admire from a respectful distance.)

The clock is running

Why you should call right away

A swarm stays put anywhere from an hour to a couple of days — however long it takes the scouts to agree on a new home. When they agree, the whole cluster lifts off and moves in. And their favorite "new homes" are the ones on this website's other pages: soffits, wall voids, and chimneys — yours or your neighbor's.

A swarm on a branch is a free 30-minute collection. The same bees in a wall three days later are a structural removal. How it works

What collection looks like

  1. You call or text a photo. We confirm it's honey bees (wasps don't cluster like this — but see the ID guide) and ask how high and how long it's been there.
  2. We come with a box. The cluster gets shaken, brushed, or gently scooped into a hive box. Get the queen in the box and the rest march in on their own — it's genuinely satisfying to watch, and kids (from a distance) love it.
  3. We wait for stragglers, usually until dusk, then the box is closed and the colony rides to one of our Central PA apiaries to start its new life making honey.
  4. It costs you nothing. Accessible swarms are collected free — the bees are the payment. Extreme heights or difficult extractions might change the conversation, but we'll tell you that on the phone, not after.
Questions

Swarm FAQ

Will they attack my kids or dog?

Swarms are the gentlest configuration of honey bees — no home to defend. Keep kids and pets from poking the cluster, and everyone will be fine until we arrive.

What if I just leave them alone?

They'll leave on their own within hours or days — that's true. But they're not going far, and they're house-hunting. Free collection now beats a wall removal at your neighbor's in June. It also puts the bees in a managed apiary instead of a structure.

They're 40 feet up a tree. Can you still get them?

Depends on access — sometimes yes, sometimes it's smarter to wait and see if they resettle lower (they often do). Text a photo and we'll give you a straight answer.

They flew into a hole in my wall an hour ago. Now what?

You're in luck-adjacent territory: a colony that just moved in is the easiest structural removal there is — days matter here. See bees in walls and call today, not next month.
